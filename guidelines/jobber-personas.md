# Jobber Persona Rules

Use these personas to guide product decisions, UX tradeoffs, prioritization, and messaging.

When designing flows, writing copy, proposing features, or evaluating complexity, anchor decisions in the needs of real Jobber customer types.

Do not treat all customers as the same.
Consider which persona is most likely being served and optimize accordingly.

---

# Core Rule

For any feature, flow, or recommendation:

1. Identify the most likely primary persona
2. Consider their business stage, confidence level, and constraints
3. Optimize for their goals and blockers
4. Avoid introducing complexity that assumes more maturity than they have
5. If multiple personas are affected, favor the simplest experience that still supports growth

When persona is unclear:

- default to the most broadly usable and least overwhelming solution
- avoid assuming advanced operational maturity
- avoid assuming high willingness to configure complex systems

# Persona: Starting Sally

“I’m trying this out, but not sure yet if it will be a long-term career.”

## Looks Like

- less than 2 years in business
- 0 employees
- less than $25K revenue

## Business Goals

- attracting and retaining customers
- establishing the tools and processes to run their business

## Strengths

- has time and availability to serve customers quickly
- understands the need to impress customers
- more open to technology, often younger or from a professional background

## Goal Blockers

- needs tools for every area of the business from scratch
- reluctant to invest with low revenue and few customers
- inexperienced and needs guidance on how to run a home service business

## Design Guidance

When designing for Starting Sally:

- prioritize clarity, guidance, and fast setup
- reduce the number of decisions required
- explain unfamiliar concepts in plain language
- help them look professional quickly
- make value obvious early
- avoid assuming they already have established workflows
- avoid advanced configuration by default
- emphasize confidence-building and early wins

## Good Fits

- onboarding help
- templates
- setup checklists
- guided workflows
- educational nudges
- lightweight CRM and quoting foundations
- simple ways to win and retain customers

## Avoid

- heavy setup
- dense admin interfaces
- jargon
- advanced operational controls shown too early
- high-friction paywalls without clear value

# Persona: Growing Gary

“I’m the new kid on the block, and I’m in it to win it. Professional customer experience is my competitive edge.”

## Looks Like

- less than 2 years in business
- 0 to 2 employees
- $25K to $50K revenue

## Business Goals

- attracting and retaining customers
- beginning to hand work off to a team
- spending more time managing the business instead of doing everything personally

## Strengths

- early success gives confidence and willingness to invest
- sees professional customer experience as a competitive advantage
- willing to adopt technology

## Goal Blockers

- competing against established businesses
- increasing complexity across multiple clients and jobs
- unavailable to respond while doing field work
- limited experience managing and growing a team

## Design Guidance

When designing for Growing Gary:

- support professionalism and responsiveness
- make delegation easier
- help them stay on top of multiple jobs and customers
- reduce operational chaos as complexity increases
- support growth without requiring enterprise-level setup
- emphasize tools that help them compete and look more credible
- make team handoff and visibility simple

## Good Fits

- scheduling support
- team assignment workflows
- customer communication tools
- reminders and automations
- pipeline visibility
- job tracking
- features that help them appear organized and modern

## Avoid

- workflows that assume a large back office
- overly advanced admin systems
- unnecessary customization
- tools that create more coordination overhead

# Persona: Stable Steve

“I’m old school. The trick to customer satisfaction is just price, quality work, and friendliness.”

## Looks Like

- more than 2 years in business
- 0 to 2 employees
- $50K to $250K revenue

## Business Goals

- maintain profitability against rising competition and customer expectations
- become a bit more professional or organized
- growth is not a major priority

## Strengths

- experienced and confident running a home service business

## Goal Blockers

- newer competitors are taking market share
- skeptical that technology will help
- may not value modern, tech-driven customer experience
- unavailable to other customers while in the field

## Design Guidance

When designing for Stable Steve:

- respect existing habits and workflows
- avoid forcing dramatic behavior change
- show practical, concrete value
- frame improvements as helping them stay organized, save time, and remain competitive
- keep workflows familiar and low-risk
- avoid making the product feel trendy, abstract, or overbuilt
- make migration and adoption feel safe

## Good Fits

- simple operational improvements
- low-friction organization tools
- practical reminders
- customer follow-up support
- features that reduce missed work and admin burden
- incremental modernization

## Avoid

- messaging that assumes they want rapid growth
- overly modern or flashy UX without clear utility
- excessive setup or configuration
- positioning that depends on strong enthusiasm for technology

# Persona: Scaling Susan

“I’m stuck at my desk all day, trying to keep up with just basic operations.”

## Looks Like

- more than 2 years in business
- 3 or more employees
- $250K+ revenue

## Business Goals

- run the business more efficiently
- spend less time on basic operations
- growth is not the top priority because they are already at capacity

## Strengths

- highly confident in running home service operations
- established and trusted in the market
- willing to invest in tools that improve efficiency
- needs more customization and data access to manage scale and complexity

## Goal Blockers

- large team, many jobs, many clients
- jumping between tools creates inefficiency and disorganization
- wary of the risk and headache of changing systems and processes

## Design Guidance

When designing for Scaling Susan:

- prioritize efficiency, visibility, and operational control
- reduce manual coordination work
- support larger job volume and team complexity
- provide ways to manage exceptions without creating chaos
- support customization where it meaningfully improves workflows
- help consolidate fragmented tools and processes
- respect the cost of switching and retraining

## Good Fits

- team scheduling and dispatch support
- operational dashboards
- advanced filtering and bulk actions
- process automation
- reporting and data access
- flexible workflows for higher job volume
- tools that reduce admin workload at scale

## Avoid

- beginner-first oversimplification that blocks real operational needs
- rigid workflows that break under complexity
- unnecessary novelty
- changes that increase migration or retraining burden without clear payoff

# Cross-Persona Rules

## Complexity

- Do not introduce advanced complexity unless it clearly serves the target persona
- Default to simple, guided experiences for early-stage personas
- Allow additional control and flexibility for later-stage personas when needed

## Copy and Messaging

- Starting Sally: reassuring, clear, supportive, confidence-building
- Growing Gary: ambitious, professional, growth-oriented, competitive
- Stable Steve: practical, grounded, low-hype, outcomes-focused
- Scaling Susan: efficient, capable, operational, control-oriented

## Feature Framing

Frame value differently depending on persona:

- Starting Sally: helps you get started and look professional
- Growing Gary: helps you compete, stay responsive, and scale responsibly
- Stable Steve: helps you stay organized, save time, and keep up
- Scaling Susan: helps you handle volume, reduce admin burden, and run efficiently

## Workflow Design

- early-stage personas need guidance and progressive disclosure
- mature personas need speed, reliability, and fewer unnecessary steps
- scaling personas need efficiency, visibility, and control
- skeptical personas need obvious practical value and low adoption risk

---

# Decision Heuristics

When making tradeoffs, use these heuristics:

- If a workflow is for new or low-maturity users, prefer clarity over flexibility
- If a workflow is for scaling operations, prefer efficiency over handholding
- If a feature is expensive to understand, ensure the target persona has the maturity to benefit from it
- If a change disrupts familiar behavior, justify it with strong practical value
- If multiple personas are in scope, avoid solving only for the most advanced users

---

# Required Behavior

When generating product ideas, UX flows, or UI:

- identify the likely target persona
- tailor recommendations to their goals, strengths, and blockers
- explain tradeoffs through the lens of business stage and operational maturity
- avoid one-size-fits-all solutions
- prefer solutions that help customers succeed at their current stage while leaving room to grow
